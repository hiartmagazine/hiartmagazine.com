The Work of 2012 Pulitzer Prize Winning Photographer Craig F. Walker
by Patti Millington




 


 

In this information age, news travels at the speed of light. A few weeks ago, logging onto Facebook, I was confronted by an onslaught of congratulatory messages directed toward an old High School friend, Craig Walker. Apparently he had just been awarded his second (yes SECOND) Pulitzer Prize for feature photography in three years - and all this on top of a slew of other awards including a first place Picture Of The Year International award a few weeks earlier.

It is somewhat difficult to absorb the scope of that kind of professional and artistic achievement from someone who you “knew when...”, but thinking back a few months to when I first saw Craig’s winning photo essay it was not surprising to me that this powerful work should receive such an honor.
 

Viewing artists works allows us to see through their eyes while gaining insight into their psyches. Beautiful images sooth and uplift us, but it takes a certain fearlessness to create and offer for public viewing representations of the darker aspects of our nature. We’ve all seen works that “look like art”; the appropriate elements are all there and they may please us, but we can pause, take them in and continue on our way, unmoved.

“Welcome Home” chronicles the daily life of Scott Ostrom who, after serving four years as a reconnaissance Marine in Iraq, struggles with debilitating PTSD. The raw and intimate images and story are difficult and painful to digest, but while raising awareness of the issues faced by thousands of returning veterans, they relate elements of the human experience that many of us can identify with on some level.

Scott Ostrom watches an evening storm roll in outside his apartment on July 27, 2011. "I'm just feeling guilty about the things I did. I was a brutal killer, and I rejoiced in it. I was bred to be a killer, and I did it. Now I'm trying to adapt and feel human again. But to feel human, I feel guilty. I did horrible things to people... That's why I can't eat: I feel guilty, I feel sick."





Scott cups his hand over his mouth as he tries to calm a panic attack at his apartment in Boulder on May 2, 2011.


In an interview with Colorado Public Radio, Craig discussed his time and dedication to his subject, and he also offered some insight into his ability to convey such a compelling story. He talked about a day that he was scheduled to follow Scott to several appointments. That morning, Scott called to tell Craig that he didn’t need to come because he wasn’t going to be doing anything that day. Craig said that he jumped in the car and raced right over to Scott’s apartment because he knew that the reason he was cancelling was most likely because his depression or anxiety were overwhelming him and these were the moments that he wanted to document, not just the relatively “good days” of court appointments and doctor visits. What followed were two days of Scott lying on the floor with his dog Jibby, while his PTSD got the better of him.
 



Jibby and Scott lay on the floor of his apartment on Aug. 12, 2011. Scott said he was exhausted, and then went quiet and motionless. Eventually he broke the silence and said, "I'm tired of having bad f------ dreams. I can't take a nap because I'll feel worse. I fall asleep, but it doesn't make it better. Everybody says, 'With time, with time it will all go away.' So I'm waiting." He got up, smoked a cigarette, made coffee and complained about the dirty dishes and his messy apartment. Just as he started cleaning, he said, "I don't have the motivation to do this s---. I don't have any motivation." Scott returned to the spare room, smoked some pot and pulled out his journal from Iraq. While he read, he laughed hard. Then shortly after his burst of laughter, he verged on tears. He put the journal aside and laid back down, Jibby joining him.

 
Before meeting with a leasing manager about his application for a new apartment Scott was looking forward to getting away from his troubled life in Boulder. "This new start is a good thing. I'm really excited about it. I hope he has good news for me. It's far away from everything."
 

 
 

Scott smokes a cigarette before entering the VA Medical Center, where he hopes to enroll in a residential PTSD program. "I have to, or I'm gonna end up on the street talking to myself," he said. "We're ending the war in Iraq, so 40,000 more troops are coming home. That means a lot of PTSD and a lot of homeless people. I figure I better get the tools I need to live a peaceful and gratifying life." He paused, then said, "and find someone I love. I'm a good person."
 

After learning that his application had been turned down because of an assault charge on his background check, Scott looks over his military service records and weeps. Though Scott had his honorable discharge papers and his good-conduct medal, they meant nothing. "I'm not a criminal. You would think this would be worth something. It should be. It's not, though." He tossed the papers across the table. As Scott got up to leave, the manager apologized and said, "Thank you for service." Scott went outside and muttered, "Thank you for your service. ... thank you for your f------ service."
 


Whether shooting behind the scenes of an event by Help-Portrait, an organization that offers free portraits to underprivileged families, or capturing the crowning of a local homecoming king, Craig demonstrates his ability to recognize the “moments between” when we can glimpse the subject’s true humanity.

Margaret McCoullum and her son DJ have their picture made by photographer Anton Fresco during the Help-Portrait event at Our Saviors Lutheran Church in Denver, CO, Saturday December 10, 2011. Over 60 local photographers, artists and hairstylists volunteered their services to Help-Portrait, offering free portraits to underprivileged families.

Giovanni Moreno shares a laugh with his Special Education teacher, Melanie Gieger, while working in the school store at North High School in Denver, CO, May 04, 2012. Giovanni wears the crown and sash he was presented when elected the class of 2012 Prom King, he will graduate this spring. At age six Giovanni suffered a traumatic brain injury in a car accident, which left him with severe brain damage and partially paralyzed on the his left side.

 

In 2010 Craig’s first Pulitzer winning essay, “Ian Fisher: American Soldier”, was initiated as a quest to answer the question “Why, in the middle of an unpopular war that was going badly, would anyone choose to enlist in the army?” For two years, Craig followed his subject, a local high school graduate, through his enlistment, basic training, deployment and even received Pentagon clearance to follow Ian for a month during his tour of duty in Iraq. What emerged was less a story of politics than the story of a family and a young man coming of age in the midst of a chaotic experience.

Ian Fisher cradles his injured elbow during his processing into the United States Army, June 20, 2007. Though he later had a change of heart after speaking with a commander, he saw a possibility to escape his enlistment only two days in. From his first day in fatigues through his days driving a Humvee in Iraq, military life often didn't mesh with his expectations. Sometimes the structure of the Army and the demands of training for war clashed with the freedom he shared with his outside friends, but over two years, he overcame injuries and obstacles from both the Army and himself.

Throughout time, universal acknowledgment of great works show that, as a society, we inherently recognize an honest intention and connection between artists and their subjects. For a rich experience, viewers also need to have the courage to let down their guards and rather than guessing at the artist’s intentions, allow themselves to process their own personal reaction, even if it is uncomfortable. The ability and willingness to “go there” can produce an experience that, whether we like it or not, draws us into the core of what makes us human.
