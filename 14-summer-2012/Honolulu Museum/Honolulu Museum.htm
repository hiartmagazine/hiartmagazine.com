by Michael Shewmaker


A George Rickey masterpiece of kinetic sculpture now rests on the front lawn of the Honolulu Museum of Art. On loan from the Federal Government while they renovate its permanent location, the piece consists of two oblong, counterbalanced stainless steel frames that swing and sway in the slightest breeze, they slowly cross, intersect and diverge always capturing a different view of the stately palms above. Their dance above me during a recent visit was oddly suggestive of the similar dance underway within the walls of the museum at its feet.

 
The Honolulu Academy of Art and The Contemporary Museum have now become the Honolulu Museum of Art. This marriage, the brainchild of Thurston Twigg Smith, a founder of the Contemporary, was first proposed more than ten years ago, the economic downturn spurring its consummation. It was a difficult meld to put together when so many diverging interests were represented by the former institutions. The possibility (and the dream) exists that the future of a single, strong body will outshine what the two former parts ever were.


 

The merger of the two museums actually has taken the form of a bequest. The Contemporary Museum has donated all of its assets and collection to the Honolulu Museum, the TCM Board Members now sit on the Honolulu Museum Board (a whopping 42 member body) and all of the employees were retained and are now Honolulu Museum employees.
Nearly concurrent with the merger was the selection of a new Director. The Museum (then the Academy) chose Stephan Jost, who came to Honolulu from a recent five year tenure at the Shelburne Museum in Vermont. Jost took the helm in May of 2011, The merger/gift of the Contemporary took place in July and as of March, this year the name is now the Honolulu Museum of Art. Mr. Jost has been a very busy man.

 

Jost is a dynamic captain who has a clear vision of where he wants to steer his new ship. The result will be a very different institution than the Academy of old. Although most local people are familiar with our museum, market research made it clear that visitors to the islands were largely unaware of its existence. "What's in a name?" A great deal, we will soon learn. Long overdue, this correction will be cathartic and already has charged the organization with a much-needed energy.

The museum has over a hundred twenty full time employees, eighty part time employees, a budget of ten to twelve million dollars and more than eight thousand members. Although not threatened financially the museum has been running at a serious deficit, daily losses averaged $3,911 over the past few years. Stemming this tide is essential for a stable future.

Spalding House (the former Contemporary) will gradually shift to performing a more educational role. The gift shop there has closed, a smaller shop will re-open near the Caf√© on the lower level. Visitors will still be able to enjoy the lush gardens and unique galleries of the Makiki Heights location.

The main galleries on Beretania are undergoing some changes. Galleries 1 through 8; you know them, the beautiful, if a bit stayed, rooms that housed the chronological history of western art. No more! They are being transformed into thematic galleries with brightly colored walls and interesting juxtapositions of periods. The goal is a more dynamic viewing experience that holds the visitor, introduces them to a broader conception of what works as art and makes the whole experience more enjoyable. Galleries 27 and 28 in the Luce Pavilion will now feature contemporary exhibitions.
The ban on photography within the museum has been lifted. Photographing art in museum settings has been a controversial issue and will continue to be. The protection of copyright, prevention of damage from flash and interruption of viewer experience being some of the concerns. The fact is that everyone carries a camera on their person at this point and denying them the right and pleasure of sharing their experience is not only small minded but bad for the business of curating art. Jost has succeeded in lifting this ban. Bring your camera and fire away!
The new Honolulu Museum of Art is rapidly evolving and will be permanently transformed by the current turn of events. Just how things will change remains to be seen but we hope that a more responsive, more relevant and more utilized institution is in our future.
