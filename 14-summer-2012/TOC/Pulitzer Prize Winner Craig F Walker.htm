The Work of 2012 Pulitzer Prize Winning Photographer Craig F. Walker
 
In this information age, news travels at the speed of light. A few weeks ago, logging onto Facebook, I was confronted by an onslaught of congratulatory messages directed toward an old High School friend, Craig Walker. Apparently, he had just been awarded his second (yes SECOND) Pulitzer Prize for feature photography in three years - and all this on top of a slew of other awards including a first place Picture Of The Year International award a few weeks earlier.

It is somewhat difficult to absorb the scope of that kind of professional and artistic achievement from someone who you “knew when…”, but thinking back a few months to when I first saw Craig’s winning photo essay it was not surprising to me that this powerful work should receive such an honor.
 
“Welcome Home” chronicles the daily life of Scott Ostrum who, after serving four years as a reconnaissance Marine in Iraq, struggles with debilitating PTSD. These raw and intimate images and story are difficult and painful to digest, but while raising awareness of the issues faced by thousands of returning veterans, they relate elements of the human experience that many of us can identify with on some level.
Scott watches an evening storm roll in outside his apartment on July 27, 2011. "I'm just feeling guilty about the things I did. I was a brutal killer, and I rejoiced in it. I was bred to be a killer, and I did it. Now I'm trying to adapt and feel human again. But to feel human, I feel guilty. I did horrible things to people... That's why I can't eat: I feel guilty, I feel sick."
Viewing artists works allows us to see through their eyes and alternately to gain insight into their psyches. Beautiful images sooth and uplift us, but it takes a certain fearlessness to create and offer for public viewing representations of the darker aspects of our nature. We’ve all seen works that “look like art”; the appropriate elements are all there and they may please us, but we can pause, take them in and continue on our way, unmoved. Whether shooting behind the scenes of an event by Help-Portrait, an organization that offers free portraits to underprivileged families, or capturing the crowning of a local homecoming king, Craig demonstrates his ability to recognize the “moments between” when the subject’s true humanity shines through. The cover photo of “Welcome Home” captures Scott Ostrum in the midst of a full-fledged panic attack and is reminiscent of Edvard Munch’s “The Scream”, a disturbing and enduring image that has prompted endless analysis to the point of becoming a modern pop icon.
