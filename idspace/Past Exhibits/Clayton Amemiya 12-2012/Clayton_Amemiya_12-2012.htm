Iga

of the Mind





vessels by
 
Clayton Amemiya

 
 
     
€

 

  
€

The vessels in this exhibition are traditional Asian shapes, Ohia wood-fired in my anagama (tunnel kiln) in upper Waiakea-Uka. "Iga" refers to a ceramic producing area of Japan near Kyoto that specialized in vessels for chanoyu (tea ceremony), the best of which date to the 1500's when powerful lords fought each other in their attempts to unify the country. These unsettling times stimulated the craftsmen to produce some of the most memorable pots of the era. Characteristically, vessels were multi-fired, the wood fuel producing areas of natural ash glaze and burnt marks on the surfaces. My pots were produced in the spirit of Iga.

- Clayton Amemiya

Twelve years ago, following decades as a professional potter studying the arcane techniques and methodologies of our near extinct alchemy, I walked into the studio of Clayton Amemiya and saw a vessel the likes of which I'd never seen before.

 
€

The object seemed to float in its own private space on the table.

 
€

It wasn't unstable, but well grounded. It was neither symmetrical, nor truly asymmetrical, neither really centered in the bilateral way potters understand products of the wheel, nor perceptibly lop-sided.

 
€  

The glaze was similarly mystical. It was sophisticated, yet raw and primitive.

 
€

There was a sense of great purpose to the vessel, belied by an extraordinary informality. It was balanced - an object still mobile in space and time.

- Stephen Freedman
