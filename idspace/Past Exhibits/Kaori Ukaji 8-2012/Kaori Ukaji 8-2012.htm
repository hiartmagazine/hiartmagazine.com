Two decades ago at an annual community exhibition at one of our local art centers I was confronted by a pair of immense red ovals emblazoned upon fabric attached awkwardly to the ceiling. I tried to walk away, but the strange otherworldliness of the diptych riveted me to the floor.

 
€

Each year at these exhibitions, another and another work by the same artist would appear, unexpectedly punctuating otherwise predictable collections of traditional painting, sculpture and ceramics. The pieces were never pretty, romantic or easy. They were always challenging and uncomfortable yet deeply moving.
 
€

I finally found opportunity to speak with the artist, Kaori Ukaji, about one of her works – a sixty foot long field of glistening graphite on paper draped down a wall and across the entire diameter of the gallery. I characterized the work as minimalist in nature. She was appalled. “Minimal? No! There is so much information,” she exclaimed.
 
€
I looked more closely at the seamless system of twilight grays and realized that the entire surface of the work had been rendered using a graphite pencil, a months-long process inculcating thousands upon thousands of individual marks upon a work of such profound complexity that it dizzied consciousness.

 
€
Our gallery, idspace exists to offer venue to visionary artists of our community, local or global. Artists like Kaori Ukaji are the reason we exist.

　
Stephen Freedman
