EDITORIAL
HI Art Magazine - America N.F.S

 

  
  When we visit an art exhibition, at least one piece often bares the ubiquitous sign, "N.F.S." In our society today, the idea of something occupying commercial space, yet being 'Not For Sale' is an anomaly. Usually it means that the object in question was already sold to someone and is now just on loan for exhibit. More rarely, the piece of art is so personal, the artist doesn't want to sell it.

Maybe that's the attraction of museums: things not for sale - things so much a part of our history and culture that they have value, yet are beyond sale. But usually we pay a fee to enter museums or at least are solicited for donations; so at least attendance is for sale. It's capitalism at work! The founding economic system of our country was capitalism. Feudal lords didn't run America as they'd once run Europe. Common people weren't excluded from access to wealth. That was our freedom. Democracy had been at work since the Magna Carta overthrew the monarchy in England. Grass roots capitalism, however was seeded in America.

Here everything is for sale! The notion was that everyone had access to the government, so access to money and power was for all. But during the last century a popular revolution occurred as mass media developed. Music, politics, art… almost everything you could think of became merchandize, to be sold to as many people as possible through a disseminating media machine that could sell just a small part of a persona or idea, to a large population.

When I first arrived in America in 1965, there was an underground music scene evolving - people you could only hear on extreme radio. New voices arose and they were fresh and threatening to the establishment. Names like Jimi Hendrix or Janis Joplin or Bob Dylan were part of a reaction against America For Sale. But the youth of America wanted it, and so, in the end it found its way to the market. Once it became clear that there was money in rebellion, nothing new could emerge without it being immediately sponsored, packaged and merchandized to a mass market for profit.

In 1979, an actor - and not a very good one - ran for president in a democracy where everyone had a choice. During his campaign Ronald Reagan proclaimed that it was time to reduce national controls on pollution emissions since smog in the state of California he governed, had been 'substantially controlled'. Coincidently, that day the front page of the L.A. Times was adorned with an image of the tips of a few buildings emerging from a gray cloud, subtitled with Reagan's quote.

Yet he won. It didn't make sense. But it wasn't about democracy, it was about capitalism. The presidency was a marketable commodity that was for sale through good advertising. Television had become the medium that sold the position. Of course an actor would be the perfect choice to fill the niche.

Twenty years later, when George W. Bush attacked Iraq he said 'They attacked us first!' Yet there were no Iraqis on the plane that flew into the Twin Towers. 'We have to fight back,' he insisted. 'Why not attack Paraguay?' some might have wondered. But the war had been purchased with hard earned media dollars. Public opinion had been bonded and secured with federal money and was being spent in the service of our founding principle: the making of profit.

Something insidious has happened to the country of opportunity, where once original voices were heard, and innovative minds from around the world came for venue. The principle upon which the country was founded - not the one we sell, democracy - but the one we buy, capitalism, has created a nation in which everything is for sale.

Politicians in the 50's and 60's, told Americans that the great enemy of our democracy was Communism, now it's Terrorism. We've often missed the distinction that democracy is a political system and communism is an economic system, not theoretically in conflict. Today terrorism is a military strategy, neither a political system, nor and economic one.

From all this rhetoric you might think that I must be a communist or perhaps even a terrorist, but I'm not. I'm an evolutionist. You probably didn't think that 'evolutionism' was a political system like democracy, or an economic system like communism, or even a strategy like terrorism, but maybe it's all three:
In biology today, we no longer understand Charles Darwin's principle of natural selection as 'survival of the fittest,' some sort of socio-economic battleground in which the most aggressive dominate. Today we understand that evolution of life is rather 'the survival of the best fit.' At any given place or time, a principle or adaptation that once worked well for an organism, might cease to be effective and even cause its demise when the environment changes.

Competition as a principle has limits: The cheetah, for example, fastest animal on earth is competitively adapted to breed, hunt and kill, but only on the run. In our modern world, that preeminent competitive adaptation, so successful once, has doomed that species to extinction. In zoos the cheetah can hide but it can't run. On the other hand, powerful species like ants have succeeded for millions of years upon cooperative principles. They have become competitive between species by limiting competition within their species.

Humans, uniquely have harnessed the dichotomous principles of high competition and high cooperation within and between species to our advantage. We are the most cooperative of primates, and the also the most competitive. We adapt to times and places to distinguish the appropriate circumstances for each strategy. As we see in family, sports, and even war, friends become enemies and enemies become friends at advantageous times. Emblemized politically, capitalism might exemplify competition while communism epitomizes cooperation. The brief political history of this century has shown us that if we compete too heavily we become isolated by our aggression. The fall of communism in the last one demonstrated that if we compete too little we are overwhelmed by the motivated.


So, as humans we live an oscillating system of cooperation and competition, adapting to time and place, often alternating strategies regularly. But as with the cheetah, when we lean upon a canalized principle such as unlimited competitive capitalism for too long, we find ourselves unable to adapt. Within our country, public opinion is purchased for profit. By selling ourselves questionable information we isolate our country internationally, and ultimately render ourselves as fragile as the storied cheetah.

The few who control the sales media have become modern 'feudal lords' who have bought and sold democracy to a nation of indentured servants. Half a century ago in America it seemed important ethically that people sold you something valuable. Now it seems important just to close a sale. If you can't afford a product, then someone will sell you the money to buy it. It's called credit. You pay it off with your future.

In this country, freedom was supposed to be 'Not For Sale.' But in the museum which our culture has become, the N.F.S. sign beside freedom, now just means someone already bought it, and it's now just here for exhibit.
