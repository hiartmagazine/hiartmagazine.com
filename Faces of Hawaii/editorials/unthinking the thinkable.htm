EDITORIAL

 
 
From William Blake to Vincent Van Gogh, creativity and insanity have always been storied cousins. A recent study associating creativity with mental illness (Peterson, Carson and Higgins 2003) suggests that stressor hormones cause the brain to lower 'latent inhibition'. Latent inhibition is described as the tendency of the brain to limit examination of previously categorized information. So, in stressful situations, with reduced latent inhibition, we are triggered to reconsider our assumptions about fixed categories, opening our minds to the creative possibility of finding new solutions to problems offered by our changing environment.

Any form of creativity demands temporarily ceasing to accept things as they are, and instead seeing things as they might be. If we identify a tree branch fallen in our path, as simply a stick, it might be stepped over, barely noticed. If we are starving and fruit is in a high tree, our stick might be renamed a picker. If we are cold, it might become kindling, or a weapon to acquire a bear's skin which might, itself be renamed our fur coat.

When environments remain stable, the effective mind relegates the many familiar elements of that circumstance to the area of 'given.' Special attention is reserved for unfamiliar and novel stimuli. Under stress, assumptions of irrelevance are suspended, and accepted categories of reality are reexamined, becoming elements in a creative process. We reconsider our assumptions, in the hopeful service of reorganizing them in some effective way designed to meet our new challenges.

The origins of this process may be found in natural selection. The history of life is peopled with 'hopeful monsters' – mutations offering new adaptations – launched out into evolving systems. Usually new variations fail, but occasionally they survive, offering opportune innovations. In human affairs creative concepts are launched into our social environs. Most innovations are rejected, but (especially in changing times) thought innovations which offer adaptive interpretations of our reality are adopted and become part of the evolving social milieu. Transformation occurs.

In the processes of art making, as in the emergence of life itself, patterns are built and replicated with critical imperfection. Those magical variations instigate innovation. In the emotional contagion initiated by shaman, artist or court jester, in performance or exhibition, thought mutations offer optional interpretation of our perceived human accord.

Under stress the brain struggles to reinterpret the deterministic patterns of cultural consensus. A two-edged sword of creativity swings through the open human mind, destroying assumptions, unthinking the thinkable then thinking the unthinkable. An avalanche of information assaults us. The dice roll, and our own hopeful monsters – mutations of thought experiments – offer up innovative visions, constructing new paradigms for survival or demise in our ever-changing reality.
