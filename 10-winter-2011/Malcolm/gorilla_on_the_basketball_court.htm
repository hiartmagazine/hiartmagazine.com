Most viewers instructed to view a video and count the number of times a basketball is passed between two teams, failed to notice a large man in a gorilla suit walk across the screen, beat his chest, then walk off screen.

Within the gorilla suit is Israeli sculptor, Malcolm Susman.

 

In 1983, a friend introduced me to Malcolm, a recent immigrant to Los Angeles. He told me he’d been a professional artist, until his marriage ten years earlier. By necessity, he’d become a plant production manager in Israel, and raised a family. Then the charade which had become his professional life broke down. Malcolm abandoned home and family. Arriving in the City of Angels, he came to my studio to rent space, vowing to make sculpture for the rest of his life: Another statistic on the list of California dreamers.

 


Pas de Deux

  
Like an animal released from a cage he began prolific production of massive architectural scale steel sculpture. The sweetness of a romantic soul mixed with Semitic symbolism in bold totems of welded, beaten steel. When I commented on his bifurcated forms Malcolm for the first time spoke of his childhood, and of his brother, Martin: Malcolm and his twin brother were abandoned by their parents in the course of a violent custody battle.

 

Begun in England, born of parents in custodial conflict, he and his fraternal twin brother became wards of the court and bounced from abusive orphanages to boarding schools for the first nine years of their lives. But from earliest cognizant memory, Malcolm built, crafted, and drew with a manifest destiny: As tumultuous as his life story became, a parallel story emerged, of art making, its content as benign as his life situation became malignant, following a determined, thoroughly independent course sustained by its own ethers.


At age 10, reunited with their biological father, Martin and Malcolm continued to be abused, now by family, for several more years until they were separated for the first time. Martin, left for Israel, while Malcolm went to art school in England. Then Malcolm married and followed Martin to Israel.
 


Not 2  Not 1


 


Our Separate Ways

  
So, by 1984, within my studio, columns towered in intractable steel, formed to curl like blades severing air, hanging balanced, free from supporting hand. Hideous, shrieking, grinding of steel shaped faces linked in fuming welds, as the rancorous hammer pounded. A thousand hours later, Apollonian forms balanced effortlessly in ether, poised between worlds, shrouding roiling, tumultuous interiors, always a balance consummated between immaculate complimentary twin parts.
 


Ha Ha Ha

 

On the psychologist’s test screen, two teams of basketball players pass the ball back and forth. The audience of average human guinea pigs, attempts to count the number of passes between the people clad in blue. In the midst of all this, that man in the gorilla suit strolled out on the court and pounded his chest, then walked off. Most of the viewers, embroiled in their task of counting, failed to notice this amazing event.


 

In the clamorous starlit City of Angels, where thousands struggle to imitate art, little is what it appears to be, and authenticity is rarely noticed. For a decade I watched that which passed for art, transact between dealers and collectors. In the midst of the chaos, from the corner of my eye, I noticed a man in a gorilla suit step onto the court and pound his chest. Few others noticed, but there he was, larger than life, an authentic artist, the man in the gorilla suit, Malcolm Susman, an artist, just what he appeared to be.
