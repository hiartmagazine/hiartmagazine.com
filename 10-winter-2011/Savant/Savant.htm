I am not much interested in hero worship. In my pantheon of idols I house more holy moments than great people. I recall stumbling into a revival theater in Los Angeles to see Charlie Chaplin’s masterpiece ‘The Great Dictator’. In extraordinary ballet, the diminutive Chaplin portrays an inflamed Adolf Hitler bouncing a buoyant globe of our Earth off his posterior, captivating his audience with simple joy. A moment later the inflated bubble of our world bursts, and we were left riveted to the image of a weeping dictator cradling the fragments of an exploded world in his hands. John Lennon, a hero to many, earned a moment in my pantheon when, in an interview he replied to a question about his credibility: ‘I can tell a lie but I can’t sing one.’
But over a half century into my life, I finally stumbled onto a real life hero, sitting hunched alone in the far corner of a decrepit gymnasium in the tiny town of Waimea, on the Big Island of Hawaii. He’s not a politician or poet or civil rights leader – he’s a ping pong player. Here in our island outpost, Alan Kaichi is a legend in the obscure sport of table tennis. Alan has dominated ping pong in Hawaii for several decades. But as the finals of our little tournament concludes, Alan sits alone, defeated for the first time by a local club player, who now competes for the money. Alan’s eyes are dark, his mouth moves and his head nods, as if in conversation, but there is no one within a hundred feet of him. All eyes watch the final match as the small hollow plastic ball rockets back and forth between two young gladiators.
I first encountered Alan five years ago in the capital of our island community, the city of Honolulu, at the Aloha State Games, a statewide sports festival which draws athletes from throughout the state, and sometimes around the world. As a child I was something of a tennis player. Decades later, I found my second childhood in competitive sport in ping pong. At a dormitory on a local college campus I’d found a handful of skilled players against whom I could hone my game. Despite a history of aptitude for racket sports, and my penchant for competition, I began to wonder if I could ever compete with younger men who had quicker hands, fired balls at the speed of light and understood the spin and strategies which make this game much more than mini tennis. Then I saw Alan.
In the scalding tropical heat of a Honolulu midsummer’s day, I had just completed my own match in the ‘C’ division, and settled back in the bleachers to watch the top players compete in the Elite division. Our club champion stepped up to the table, stretching his prodigious calves and thighs, limbering up his guns for the match. To the other side of the table shuffled a tiny man, carrying a little too much weight on his belly, paddle in hand. He neatly folded his towel under the table and stepped onto the court, bouncing very slightly like a buoy in the waters off Diamond Head. As the warm up began, he gently blocked back each shot that came to him, offering little resistance, expression or emotion.
I double checked with the person sitting beside me. Was this really the state champion? Had I come to the wrong court? No, I was assured, this was indeed Alan Kaichi, the best player in the state of Hawaii, and had been for decades! I watched as this strange little man proceeded to pick apart our champ, blocking, lifting, slicing the ball, all the time floating easily at his end of the table, almost in slow motion, his shining black eyes revealing no secrets.
The Elite division progressed, and I followed Alan to each match, fascinated as this awkward man decimated each and every competitor who came before him. As a child might pluck the wings from a captive fly, so Alan found and exposed the weaknesses of player after player, absorbing their power, reversing their spins, handcuffing them with uncanny strategies. Intense, yet strangely emotionless his unblinking eyes appeared to capture every motion and nuance of his opponents, logging them into some arcane inner computer, retrieving relevant results in the moment before he made contact with the ball. Alan waded easily through the draw, dispatching all comers to win the championship.
Returning home I was inspired. If this marginally mobile man, only a few years younger than me, could play at this level, then I certainly could enjoy many more competitive years in this amazing game, less limited than I’d feared by stiffening joints and slowing reflexes. I returned to my own little town and began to play and practice in earnest, believing that I might find new, creative ways to approach the game. The next summer I again flew to Honolulu to compete in the state championships, and again witnessed the five foot three inch Alan elegantly dispatch the draw. I asked players from his club about this phenomenon and heard uneasy rumblings about Alan’s anti social nature, inapproachability and emotional instability. Nevertheless, I took my courage in my hands, approached him, and asked for a lesson. The daunting intimidating mask I’d seen on the court, took me in for a moment, then cracked into a friendly smile and said “Of course!”
Alan took me to an empty court at the back of the gymnasium and paddled the ball gently back and forth with me, explaining that the tiny white ball could be viewed as a clock – it could be approached at many different times. At three o’clock the ball moved toward you and could be addressed horizontally. But at one o’clock the ball might be lifted in an arcing trajectory, caused to rotate at great speed with topspin. At five o’clock that topspin might be neutralized by grazing the bottom of the flying orb. For a moment I peered into Alan’s eyes to see if he was kidding, maybe playing a joke on a novice, but he was serious. I told him that in my childhood sport of tennis there were just a handful of possible shots, each a fairly obvious skill to be learned, the rest was simply good execution and athleticism. “How many shots are there in ping pong?” I asked.
He smiled and answered easily: Not only could the ball be addressed at many ‘hours of the clock’ but it could be met as it rose off the table in ‘position A’, at its peak ‘position B’, or as it dropped, ‘position C’. It could also be struck at the heel of the paddle blade, in the center, or toward the tip, for different levels of spin and speed! A bizarre private language emerged, offering a panoply of possible ways to approach the ball, multiplying into hundreds of potential combinations! At first it all seemed an unreal invention, but as I began to practice, I began to see Alan’s clock. As the ball rotated I began to discern ‘the time to strike’, and the myriad implications and variations which came to light if I was willing to enter his world of possibilities.
Each year I made the pilgrimage to the Aloha State Games. Each year Alan was there. I’d ask for a lesson, and each year the arcane alchemy of his brilliant savant mind opened my eyes to a world of spin, footwork, and slight-of-hand deception. It was not simply the possibility of improving at the sport that intrigued me, but Alan’s luminous insight and attention to detail which hypnotized me. While stories continued to circulate about Alan’s ‘disorder’ and his periodic antisocial outbursts, I met only a gracious teacher who held the key to a magical kingdom in which physics and mathematics were shaped and formed by the hologram of a ball traveling across a table. As others shied away from what they perceived as an unpredictable eccentric, I found myself developing increasing affection, admiration and respect for him.
A year ago, at the state championships, Alan’s match demeanor appeared subtly changed. As he proceeded through the draw, he was challenged by opponents he’d previously waded through with little more resistance than a fisherman through pond reeds.
Something was different. I went to him between matches, fearful of disturbing his preparation, but as usual was met with nothing but warmth and openness.
“You seem to be playing closer to the table than usual,” I commented. Even facing powerful opponents, he was standing nearly touching the table, unwilling to relinquish the forward position or let the ball drop at all. He took each shot on the rise, anticipating trajectory and reading variable spins in the few inches available.
“I’m working on playing in position A,” he told me. Remember, position A? Taking the ball on the way up – In his coming semi final match against one of the top players he said he would play entirely that way! Why utilize only one third of his options? I watched the match, and sure enough Alan stood nearly against the table, as a dangerous, powerful opponent fired the ball at extreme speed toward him. He fielded the balls as they skidded from the table, his paddle sometimes actually moving backwards at contact in order to absorb the pace of the skidding ping pong ball. It was a long and difficult match against a player he usually handled with ease. Alan floated at table’s edge making normally easy shots far more difficult, but in an extraordinary tightrope walk, finally managed to win.
Following the match I asked Alan about the apparently bizarre strategy which appeared to limit his options so severely. He told me that he’d spent the entire year only training with beginners. He said the secret to improving against lesser competition was to ‘create situations’. He said he would hit the ball to his opponent’s strength, and move into his position of greatest vulnerability, then he’d develop the skills necessary to extricate himself. Was this why he appeared to be playing such a strange game this year? Was he just improving an aspect of his match play? “Perhaps,” he replied, but mostly it had just become ‘necessary’. With no further explanation Alan left to play the Elite finals.
He was matched against an experienced European opponent, a past Olympic coach, with a creative and varied game, but one he’d rarely lost to before. Once again Alan stood nearly touching the table, playing every shot from position A. But this cunning, persistent opponent exploited Alan’s choice and threw balls with varied spins to the very edge of the table where Alan had insufficient time to calculate the rotation. Alan lost in relatively short order, barely competing, and most astonishingly, without changing his approach to the match at any point. For a man with so many choices, he seemed to have tied one hand behind his back, playing out the match according to a predicted precept, despite that strategy’s obvious failure! I couldn’t grasp why he hadn’t even attempted to adapt. I recalled a story I’d heard years ago about an apparently paraplegic artist who painted with only his feet. When finally it was revealed that the artist actually had full use of his hands, he was asked why he chose to paint with his feet. He replied, “It ‘s the mark of a civilized man to do natural things in an unnatural way.” Apparently Alan was a highly civilized man.
A couple of years ago, a professional table tennis coach moved to our island. A committed player and brilliant analyst, the coach was able to help us local players with our flawed strokes, and due to his efforts our little isolated community had its first nationally sanctioned tournament complete with prize money – a hundred dollars for the winner. Now Alan was coming to our island to play. I warned our coach that he was in for a treat. While Alan may not have the technical skills our coach was used to seeing in the international level players he’d studied the game with, I assured him Alan would pluck apart our local talent like so many overcooked chicken wings.
People said that in his younger days Alan had been an Olympic level player, but had lacked the funds to travel, so his competition had been limited to the Hawaiian Islands, where the caliber of play was insufficient preparation for the international scene. As Alan played his warm up matches our coach shook his head. ‘This man barely moves! He’s stiff as a board. He doesn’t move his feet!’ he went on. ‘He’s steady, but look!’ he proclaimed, ‘He’s standing right up against the table!’
‘Just watch and wait,’ I chuckled recalling my own personal epiphany watching the man for the first time.
Alan was not an impressive, athlete, strong or as flashy as many of the players I’d seen even at our club, but he was incredibly creative and could readily find the weaknesses in his opponent’s games, creating shots and odd spins in awkward locations that would reduce even the most skilled and nimble of our club players to stumbling novices. He would do his magic.
In his first warm up match Alan played with one of our new players who had only competed for a few months. She normally couldn’t hit the ball over the net more than once or twice without an error. As I glanced over from where I played my own early match, something extraordinary was happening. The novice was hitting dozens of balls over the net, many of them perfectly! Following my match I checked the score sheet, and she’d actually won a game against Alan. I laughed. How kind of him! So I sat down to watch a student from a local university team play Alan next. What a match! The young man was in perfect balance on each stroke. His feet moved like lightning. Who was this kid? Why hadn’t I heard of him? He looked great! The ball appeared to be anticipating the player. Wherever he was, there was the ball, perched as if on a tee, almost still, right before him.
Nevertheless, Alan won the match handily and advanced. Next, the Elite matches began and Alan faced real competition. As in earlier matches, Alan stood with his paddle in front of the base line of the table, hovering far too close. Our coach again laughed. ‘How can he play like that?’ I just smiled and watched. As predicted Alan won the first game easily. But in the second, his opponent settled his understandable nerves at playing ‘the legend’ and served the ball deep to the base line and Alan’s middle. The strategy worked. Alan popped up an easy ball and his opponent attacked fiercely, going on to win the game. Alan had always been polite in avoiding embarrassing beginners, but he’d rarely allow a dangerous player like this one to actually win a game. I was surprised, but assumed he would now step back to where he’d be able to time the deep shots, accessing his other options. But as the next game began Alan stayed pressed to the table. He appeared to be talking to himself and he lost another game. In the fourth game, he found a way to regain control of the match, and won the next three games in short order.
I was his next opponent. While I always relished the opportunity to play the champion, in my previous matches against Alan I had always found myself in the wrong place at the wrong time, unbalanced, awkward, clumsily trying to eek out a point here and there. But today, the ball seemed to appear just where I wanted it. I was doing well! Alan had once shown me an amazing service he taught to the National Olympic Team. His paddle would rise up, then slice downward in the usual motion to create under spin. Unread, under spin will cause a ball to dip off the opponent’s paddle into the net. But in Alan’s shot, his paddle would graze the ball on his upward motion, just before the down stroke The downward stroke was all illusion, designed to mislead the opponent, who anticipating under spin, would hit the ball higher than usual, allowing the server to smack a winner.
So here came that very serve he’d shown me. Surely he wouldn’t try to use a simple deception he’d already exposed to me! I guessed he was bluffing. How cunning! I hit the ball as if it really was under spin, but the ball popped straight up in the air. Alan slapped the ball by me, and proceeded to launch the same serve at me again. Again I thought, ‘No, this time it’s just a fake! He won’t catch me on this!’ But again and again he sent the serve I knew so well straight to my forehand, and I, unable to believe he’d really give me just what I wanted, anticipated deception, and again and again found none. The ball popped straight up into the air. The spin was just what it appeared to be. Soon our match concluded predictably. Alan and I smiled, laughed and shook hands. Alan returned to the far end of the bleachers to sit with the friend who’d accompanied him to our island, and prepared for his semi final match.
One of our best players is a man trained in childhood in China. He has an incredibly powerful forehand stroke that our coach compliments as ‘professional level’. But in all his years in the islands, he had never beaten Alan Kaichi. In the several matches I’d witnessed he’d been driven to frustration, as the master tossed up strange, unpredictable spins that confused and reduced him to frustration. Even the amazing forehand didn’t work against Alan. He would take a step back and arrive at the ball just at the right angle, and easily control it back never losing even a single game. The coach watched, and I stood beside him, confident that the real Alan, like a chameleon from its skin, would now finally emerge, his vibrant new scales some amazing strategy miraculously procured from the surreal world inhabited by his mysterious muse.
Anticipating his usual defeat, our top player faced Alan, stumbling in the first game and losing easily. Despite his great forehand, his skills had diminished over the years. He’d gained weight and developed injuries which prevented him from moving with the agility which had once been his signature. Alan sent an array of mixed spins over, but insisted on staying belly-to-the-table, against hundred miles per hour forehand rockets. Floating like a butterfly, he bobbed along the base line, miraculously blocking back shot after shot. But as the second game proceeded, our club player began to read each spin Alan threw at him. The ball appeared again and again in perfect position, and his confidence burgeoned. He launched lightning fast top spin loops to the corners past Alan. His mobility miraculously improved. The ball rebounded from his paddle as if its destination was preordained. He looked magnificent, and Alan helpless. As the match continued, it was as if he’d been reborn. He was again quick and agile, his hands like lightning, his placement perfect. And then it had happened. The king had fallen! Alan was defeated.
At match end, Alan shook hands and retreated to the far end of the gymnasium and sat there muttering to the friend who had accompanied him. Everyone crowded into the bleachers as the warm up to the final match began. Our two top club players competed for the championship. Alan’s friend left him alone in the far corner of the gymnasium and came to watch. As he passed by I whispered to him, “Is Alan okay?”
“He’s having difficulty,” his friend said.

“Is he ill?” I asked.

He looked at me questioningly. “Do you know of Alan’s ‘difficulties’?” I stared blankly. I’d heard rumors before of some unspecified psychiatric problems, but knew little of fact. His friend explained that Alan was having auditory hallucinations and was best left alone for a while. I respectfully accepted the judgment, and watched as the match began.
Suddenly the Chinese player’s magnificent forehand was no longer in evidence. His feet appeared slower. The poise and perfect balance were both gone. The man who had defeated Alan was once again mortal. He moved slowly, his shots were weaker. He hit, he missed. His opponent dominated the first couple of games. But as the match progressed my attention returned again and again to Alan, sitting in the far corner of the gym, baseball cap shrouding his eyes, head dipped, lips moving as if in constant conversation. The contending players became gray shadows as Alan loomed larger and larger, his diminutive figure hunched on the edge of the bench, eyes shaded by cap brim, somehow childlike.
Between games I hesitantly made my way around the bleachers to the far end of the gym.

“Hi, Alan,” I tried, not knowing what to expect.

He looked up with a half smile, glassy eyes wide open and said hello. We spoke for a long while. At the other end of the gym, cheers announced great plays in the championship match, but I could not raise my eyes from the face before me.

Alan explained that he was hearing voices. He’d been hearing them for the past fifteen years, but recently it had become increasingly difficult to play. Medications were no longer working, and the voices had become more and more intrusive. I tried to be understanding, but awkwardly changed the subject, thanking him for being so kind during our match by hitting me shots I could return easily. Perhaps he was trying to let me win a game to spare my feelings.
Alan laughed, his eyes lighting up. “I wasn’t trying to let you win!” he said. “You were just playing great.” All of our club players were great, he said.
I suggested he’d been ‘practicing’ by hitting each player in our club just the shots we needed to look good. He disagreed. “It was the voices,” he said. It was ‘the Auditories’ he called them – telling him which shots to hit. He asked if I’d watched him playing the beginner. I said yes, and that it was very kind of him to let her win a game.
“No!” again he insisted he didn’t let her win. The voices told him which shots to hit to her, and she just grew, as if inhabited by God, able to return everything he threw at her. Again I dodged, trying to change the subject: Why was he standing close to the table? The first lesson he’d given me in the game was that choices were multiplied by positions A, B and C. Playing only position A, he was sacrificing two thirds of his options.
Alan’s eyes opened much wider and filled with tears, but he smiled. “They told me I could only play from close! I’m not allowed to move away from the table anymore. They said that if I do, every ball will drop near the net.”
Listening to the voices handcuffed his game, but he couldn’t argue with them. If he did, they simply wouldn’t shut up. He said that in the past, medications had silenced them, but in the last year medicine had stopped working. Now he heard them all the time. He explained that he had created several new services. He demonstrated by grasping the edge of his paddle and slicing the air in a near horizontal service motion, then flipping the paddle to the other side and whipping it across the middle. In an instant he snapped his wrist, and the handle of the paddle slapped back into his palm as if by magnetic force.
‘Do you know how long it took me to learn this skill?’ he asked. ‘The voices were amazed that I’d created it. I used it to defeat a high ranking national player last year, but then they told me I couldn’t use it anymore. I can teach it,” he said, “but I’m not allowed to use it in matches.” He told me that he’d developed a way to circle the ball with his paddle motion, causing the spin to reverse. Like a mime testing the insides of an invisible box, Alan’s paddle caressed an imaginary spinning orb in the ethers between us. Time slowed down as the cheering crowd at the far end of the gym rumbled like far off thunder, and Alan’s paddle formed air like the baton of a conductor of unheard music.
I reminded Alan of that state final a year ago when he had first stood against the table and been defeated by the European. Was it the voices? Yes, they had told him to use only Position A. They said he would surely lose, but they also said he would not be sad. They told him that if he tried to cry, no tears would come. Even losing, he would be happy.
“I just wish they would leave me alone so I could be normal,” he said.
But I wondered if the expression of creativity had become more important within Alan than the game itself. I wondered if the voices which prohibited the use of his creative repertoire had another agenda for him: The man who had once exposed the weakness of those around him was now demonstrating their strengths.
Alan smiled as another cheer rumbled from the finals table, barely noticed now. I thanked him for visiting our tiny club and for sharing his amazing techniques and elegant insights – for bringing out the best in us. Alan smiled, his eyes wide and his expression grateful as he shook my hand.
Suddenly rousing applause from the far end of the gym intruded. The match was over, but I couldn’t raise my eyes, even to see who had won. Alan’s black eyes gazed over at the crowd, his face appeared angelic and at peace, perhaps the voices were silent for just a moment.
“Who won?” he asked.

“I don’t know,” I replied.

“Thank you for listening to me,” he said. “Even my psychiatrist can’t really hear what I say anymore. Maybe it takes a ping pong player to understand.”
