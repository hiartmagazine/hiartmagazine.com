January at the Honolulu Academy of Arts

 

Happy holidays from the Honolulu Academy of Arts! We start a year of big changes—our search for a new director is going full steam and we hope to have an exciting announcement soon. In the meantime, our staff continues to dream up and make a reality all the innovative, engaging programming you’ve come to expect from the museum. We have nine exhibitions on view, and a packed schedule of concerts, film festivals, tours and lectures. If you’ve got some time off this month, it’s a great time to visit the Academy. We wish you a 2011 filled with peace, happiness and art!

Ongoing Exhibitions

 

Four Thousand Years of Southeast Asian Art
Henry R. Luce Gallery (28), through Jan 9
Travel through the ancient kingdoms of Thailand and Cambodia in this exhibition that includes works from the Academy’s permanent collection that have never been previously on display. Just a week left!

Graphic Cabinet 7
Very Funny!: Satire and Subversion in 18th- and 19th-Century Prints
Gallery 9, through Jan 23
Just try not to laugh out loud in Gallery 9 when you see these prints by Hogarth, Cruikshank, Daumier and Goya that poke fun at people, fashion, government and more.

Company Painting
The Jhamandas Watumull Gallery (23), through Jan 23
Among the alluring statues, jewelry and textiles hang these paintings of 19th-century everyday Indian life, people in “exotic” native costumes and flora and fauna—things that British expats in India wanted to see in the paintings they bought as souvenirs. It’s fascinating to see them in context of traditional arts.

The Tale of Genji: The Impact of Women’s Voices on a Thousand Years of Romance in the Arts
Gallery 20, through Jan 30
Guest curator Mara Miller assembled works from the Academy’s collection of texts and illustrations about the world’s first psychological novel—and most important book by a woman. Don’t miss the Edo-period Tosa School fans and album leaves painted with gold leaf (like the image above, Lady Murasaki)—they’re renowned for their depictions of gardens and seasonal “sports” such as snowman making. 

Embroidered and Embellished: The Margaret Brewer Fowler Collection
The Textile Gallery (22), through Feb 6
Spanning the globe from Morocco to India, these sumptuous furnishing, costumes, fragments and remnants are a textile legacy. The Turkish and Greek embroideries are especially impressive.

The Eight Eccentrics of Yangzhou
Maurice J. Sullivan Gallery (16), through Feb 13
The Yangzhou School often is considered to mark the beginning of modern Chinese painting. The Eight Eccentrics innovative use of color and brushwork was elaborated upon by the artists of the Shanghai School in the 19th century, and was continued into the early 20th century by many of China’s leading artists, through whom the influence of the Yangzhou School continues to the present day.

Self-Construction: The Art of Kobashi Yasuhide
Gallery 14, through Feb 20
This is your chance to rediscover a groundbreaking Japanese artist whom the New York Ties called “a Daedalian artificer” in 1961. The exhibition includes what Yasuhide called his “self-constructing sculpture”—moveable compositions that could be arranged into an every-changing variety of combinations—and groovy mid-century prints that would look right at home in Mad Men.

Designed by Shibata Zeshin (1807-1891)
Robert F. Lange Foundation Gallery (21), through Feb 20
Don’t miss this reintroduction to Japanese renaissance man Shibata Zeshin, a master painter, lacquer designer and woodblock print designer who attempted to revitalize traditional art at a time of dramatic transformation.

Meaning in Color/Expression In Line: Arman Manookian’s Modernism
John Dominis and Patches Damon Holt Gallery (29), through Feb 27
This tightly focused exhibition celebrates the arrival at the Academy of Red Sails and Hawaiian Boy and Girl (both 1928), two of the seven murals that dazzled guests at the Hotel Hana-Maui until the hotel’s sale last May. Now on long-term loan to the Academy, these magnificent paintings join a selection of Manookian's work from the museum’s permanent collection and local private collections to reveal the extraordinary talents as a painter and draftsman of this Armenian-American artist who made Honolulu his home.
