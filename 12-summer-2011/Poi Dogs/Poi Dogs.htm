by Darrell Orwig
 
Three years have rocketed by since my friends Sidney Yee and Phil Sabado asked artist friends, Pat Masumoto, Mike Takimoto and me to join them in developing a proposal for an exhibit at the Maui Arts And Cultural Center's Schaefer International Gallery. I had recently retired from there as Director.

Phil and Sid had been evolving an idea dealing with ethnicity prerogatives in an artistic discussion of things "Hawaiian". This became the basis of a long journey for all of us, individually and collectively.

The dynamics of a group show can sometimes be as engaging as the end product. Questions arise faster than answers can be found. The process is something like making plans for a building while you construct it. A major factor from the outset was finding out how like-minded we were as a group. Our friendships with each other have been long and fairly close. Still, compatibility in such an undertaking as this was an unknown quantity. The very nature of the first idea was like moving through fog. One could only see so far ahead. It really boiled down to a kind of luck of the draw. You can only anticipate so much for so long and then plunge in and see what happens.

I think back on my old civil service days and a boss I had once who described group decision making as, "Group think–it stinks". To a certain extent he had a point. The result of group effort often turns out to be intellectual mush or worse. But that's where civil policy making and artistic collaboration become two different processes. As art makers, we are by nature solitary. We may discuss a concept, agree, disagree, or agree to disagree, but when we leave the group and sit there in our individual work spaces, we are alone. And that's where the real decisions get made.


 

We were meeting monthly and eventually somebody says: "Here's what I'm going to do." And somebody else says: "I don't know what to do."

It became apparent after the first few months and a response from the exhibits committee that our first idea was mush. Enter Poi Dogs. Exit Phil (too many other commitments). Enter Nancy Skrimstad.

In spite of the group-think curse, working together seemed to be the best way to clarify our concept in our own minds, and it was. We honed in on a pair of words which opened the doors for all of us: Poi Dog. It was a perfect fit individually and collectively. We had an exhibit we could all agree on and a solid cohesive show would be the outcome.

The essence of our efforts came down to three basic issues: We're all from somewhere else, we're not who we think we are, and the intrusion or absorption of one culture by another is as old as humanity itself. Each of us in his or her own way took a personal approach based on direct experience in the present, or a historical commentary based on the past as it led to the present. The former broached two additional elements: What's in the past cannot be undone, and how responsible are we in the present for the "sins" of our fathers and ancestors? I'm sure there are volumes already written on the subject. I'm just asking the question again.

It's somewhat problematic discussing a show from the inside out, but in all honesty, I think we all stayed on track and the overall effect was a show that expressed the broad based issues we set out to address. The other side of a unifying theme is the individual diversity. We ranged from vehicles of humor to head-on serious and the enigmatic.


 

Nancy Skrimstad uses a certain level of ironic humor in her collaged mixed media wall pieces, with a turn of events installation of mixed ethnic humans inside a chain link kennel, as well as a video piece. Being of Norwegian decent, born and raised in Hawaii she asks the question: "Is it blood or heart that makes one Hawaiian?"

 

 

Pat Masumoto also takes a humorous approach in her acrylic paintings of her memories from "small kid time" growing up in a Lahaina populated with people of Hawaiian, Chinese, Filipino, Portuguese, Korean, Japanese, Caucasian, and many other backgrounds, mixed and matched like a crazy quilt in a style somewhat reminiscent of Pacific Northwest artist Gaylen Hansen.


 

Sidney' Yee's acrylic, mixed media works are painted questions reinforced by labels asking more questions. My ancestors were from China and Spain. I was born here. How Hawaiian am I? He bores into issues of cultural and racial barriers, perceived and "real'' with darkly haunting images and compositions, many in the traditions of China and Japan. His final question in the form of a multi breed dog has one complete body attempting to merge a mix of differing parts–those parts are distinctively unique. If they succeed in "blending" what will our future be like?
There are100 parts and100 faces in Mike Takimoto's installation which includes a video complete with barking dogs. The portraits are presented and framed in identically molded plastic casts, printed with black and white high-contrast faces of his friends, relatives, and students from Maui Community College where he teaches. Each picture is labeled with the person's ethnic background. There are very few singles.


 


 

For my part, a trip to London and Turkey two years ago became the source for what I perceive as a universal history of human behavior. I saw many parallels between events and myths of our Hawaii and my European ebb and flow of events. One civilization expands and absorbs another and is itself swallowed up by another. There is rarely a mutually agreed upon change; more often it is deceit and force which effect that change. When you stand on that ground, the story becomes real. The legends of Troy became the basis for an Oldenbergian giant wooden "Trojan" Poi Dog.

Trojan Chihuahuas lying in wait


 

The Elgin Marble figures lifted from the Parthenon of Athens evolved as the Poi Dog Parthenon. The question it asks: "When do we get our culture back?" The answer seems to be that we can't change history, turn back the clock, or reinvent the past, but we can make some things right.
