by Scott Yoell & Sally Lundburg
photos by Sally Lundburg & Valerie Y. O. Kim   

          

Valerie Yong Ock Kim is flutter-fast like a humming bird, quick to smile, and quick of tongue; so I was surprised that she was late to our interview. It was 9:15 am at the Waimea Coffee Company and Scott and I were prepared, notebooks in hand, equipped with coffee and pens. We waited, talked about how we would collaborate in this article, about our own artwork, about the weather. Time flew. A while later Val Kim appeared up the steps, a little displeased, mouth askew. She too had been early and waited in her car for us. Small talk ensued, treats and coffee procured, and the clouds rolled away.
 
says Val, leaning forward intently. Density, which she expresses as the dimensionless, is a word vital for this Big Island artist. Her photographic work operates in the region where personal recollection, photographic evidence and the paranormal overlap. As our talk ventured into the metaphysical, she mentions with a smile that she is a double Pisces. I googled it later, “A double-edged sword of Piscean sensitivity (Sun and Moon) may represent vulnerability… a sense of responsibility to contribute something socially, culturally, or spiritually may be pervasive. In extreme cases this may even border on martyrdom. On the more positive side, there's a finely-tuned awareness that eludes most people.” (Don McBroom – Astrological Consultations ).
 
For Val, the camera becomes the mind’s eye where both time and memory are one step removed from any definitive space, dreamy photographs become quasi-diarist investigations into perception. Influenced by her personal relationships with the mentally ill, she creates a body of work centered around the ‘transitory scapes‘ of reality. Val has close family members and friends who’ve struggled with a slow ascent to mental illness, and watched as they adapted to the knowing and not knowing every moment… almost becoming comfortable with it. Perhaps because of this, she has a keen affinity with the other side of reality.

 



 
Val Kim’s photography is broken into bodies of work titled Koi Tales; Hapai; Elements of Dreams; Tails; and Light Speed Matter. In the piece ‘Rain As Well, Pa'auilo Mauka,’ as in many of her photos, there is a blurred terrain of exotic dislocation. An ambiguity exists between what is real and the perceived.



In ‘From the Road Above the Sea, Pa'auilo Winter Morning’...a very brief glimpse into time, a time that is being determined by multiple elements of speed from the camera to the horizon, squeezed between the window of a moving car and a heavy blanket of clouds. The sugar cane is visible in a narrow yellow swath driving mauka to makai, a blurred memory of a plantation industry, long gone.

     

She first began capturing still images taking portraits of her sister, outside their home in Kahaluu, on the windward side of Oahu. “I was always shooting…starting with a Brownie camera. I had a friend that worked at Penny’s and I could develop my film there” says Val. “I wanted to go to art school, but I felt I’d get blown around.” Val was raised by a decidedly middle class Korean family (“do something practical!”) She obtained a Masters in Public Health from the University of Hawaii at Manoa, and worked from age 19 - 29 in the field of mental health, primarily in psychiatric counseling. But a personal and professional shift came, and she quit her job to work in an art gallery, and started getting side work as an extra in local film productions. Eventually this drew her away from Hawaii and into the film industry in Los Angeles, where she would work for the next 20 years. She met her husband Gerry there, a film editor, and they worked from film to film. Val often traveled home…LA to Hawaii and back, but she was never here very long.

 
 

    
     During the interview, at one point she seemed to be at loss for words.
     She began to lightly sing the lyrics of an Olomanana song Kealoha ku'u home o Kahaluu,

 
   



The song seemed to somehow contextualize her feelings of home and place, from a time both past and present.        


 
Fog rolls through Paauilo Mauka later that week as I head up to shoot photos of Val for our article. They bought this place on the Big Island a number of years ago, and began to commute back and forth from the West Coast. Gerry eventually retired, and today Val passes on the jobs that take her away from Hawaii for any extended period of time. Their home slice on the mountain is a small part of a larger compound…a green rolling property, populated with a few other homesteads. Tall norfolk pines line the long driveway as I drive in, crossing bridge, under branches, past a low stone wall. Gerry is in the garage puttering, a bit stooped. He grins roughly. He leads me to Val and I began shooting as soon as I walk in. Breakfast with Gerry at the long wooden table, then on the couch, then tucked in ferns and ginger in the garden.

 
I wanted to get a portrait of the two and glimpsed the spark in moments. He is dry, serious, expressionless, gnome-like, until a laugh breaks out. She’s nymph-like..and how old, I don’t know. A streak of grey runs down one side of her black hair, but besides that she could be 13, and then 50, and then 10. The expressions change with each shot. Our photo-shoot in her home went from kitchen, living room, outside into the meandering road, up and over the wooden bridge and then down to her little office/ studio. Val began to pull out boxes of images, and we were eventually surrounded by snapped evidence of things past and impressions of the moment.
 
“This is where I go to find inspirations,” she said. “I have a fascination with this experience of being human, being on and of the earth and at the same time, having the ability to think and sometimes see beyond this dense place.” She laughs and rolls her eyes, “Does that sound crazy?” Though Val’s practice is far from an exact science, Kim admits she is more comfortable using clinical terms and approaches to her creative practice. Influenced by artists such as Julie Taymor, Mark Berndt and Robert Irwin who are known internationally for pushing boundaries of art and perception, it is easy to see why Kim is drawn to the realms between time. She steps quite often on the edge of the paranormal when stumbling upon unexplained anomalies within her photos. In preparing new work for her upcoming show at idspace on the Big Island, she tells us she is mining the archives of her long photographic practice. Tiny little snapshots, meditations on both mortality and vitality. Pinching a small image between her fingers and holding it out to me, “I have to empty myself and look for moments of connection, where something becomes alive in this two-dimensional space.” The light darkened outside, but Val sat alone in the warm lamplight of the small room, surrounded by her work.

Valerie Yong Ock Kim is third generation, Hawaiian island-born and raised, and of Okinawan and Korean descent. She is a member of ASMP and is represented by Photo Resource Hawaii and its affiliate, STONE. She studied with Joel Meyerowitz in the Tuscany Workshops and has has exhibited in Los Angeles and Hawaii. Her work can be found in private collections throughout the US, in Sweden and Japan. She currently lives in Pa’auilo Mauka, Hawaii Island, and when not pursuing personal work, she is a Union (Local 399) Location Manager in Los Angeles. Val was writer/director on “Waging Peace,” her first documentary film, which she also co-produced. She has a Master’s Degree in Public Health at the University of Hawaii.

Scott Yoell and Sally Lundburg are members of AGGROculture, a Hawaii based art collective that creates, showcases and promotes cutting edge and challenging concepts in contemporary art. Scott is an artist engaging traditional and electronic media. His recent work has been realized in drawing, sculpture, and video/sound installation. Yoell holds a BFA from the University of Windsor, Canada, and an MFA in Imaging and Digital Arts from the University of Maryland Baltimore County, USA. Sally is an artist, photographer, and filmmaker. She has an Interdisciplinary BFA degree from The San Francisco Art Institute, and she creates work that bridges collage techniques with documentary sensibilities.
