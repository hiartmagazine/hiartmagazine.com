Dustin Miyakawa         

 

For the first time in 35 years Hawaii Craftsmen’s Raku Ho’olaule’a almost never happened. Every summer groups of potters and sculptors led by Hawaii’s top raku artists have gathered on Oahu’s east-facing shores for a weekend of raku firing, camping, music and festivities culminating in a juried exhibition at the popular downtown gallery, Mark’s Garage. But when budget cuts threatened, independent ceramic artists committed their time and resources to collectively organize and fund the event, in conjunction with its parent organization, Hawaii Craftsmen.

“We started as an all-volunteer organization and now we’ve returned to our roots,” said board member and former Hawaii Craftsmen President Jackie Mild-Lau. “There was a real spirit of help this year and everyone contributed what they could.”

 

A “ho’olaule’a” is the traditional Hawaiian gathering and Hawaii Craftsmen’s version is one of the most public and well-attended events in the islands. “Raku is like wood-firing,” said locally renowned artist and instructor Daven Hee, who led a group of students from the University of Hawaii. “It’s a communal activity that brings us together for a single purpose. But we don’t have big, wood-fired kilns here. We raku on the beach.”

Seven groups attended the 2011 event, with members from the University of Hawaii, Windward and Leeward Community Colleges, Hawaii Potter’s Guild, Jeff Chang Pottery, the Honolulu Academy of Arts and other independent studios. Each group privately funded fuel, food, transportation and equipment costs to continue what has become a tradition in Hawaii’s art scene.

 

“Raku provides best opportunity for sustaining the sense of community among the many individuals and groups that make up Hawaii Craftsmen,” said the organization’s president, Kay Mura. “For many of us, this is the one time we get to see each other throughout the year.” Hawaii’s ceramic artists agreed; this unique opportunity to collectively share in the creative experience and renew personal connections with like-minded colleagues was worth continuing.

 

Loosely translated, “raku” means “enjoyment” or “ease” and is deeply rooted in the Japanese tea ceremony. Tea master Hideyuki Yoshida from the Urasenke Foundation, an international organization dedicated to the preservation of the ceremony, juried a separate category for work designed specifically for tea. The selected works were shown alongside more contemporary pieces at the final exhibition. The contemporary category was juried by Esther Shimazu, Carl Fieber and Ken Kang, three local ceramic artists and past event organizers.

 
The Raku Ho’olaule’a embodies the spirit of both worlds. Although traditional raku firings were developed in 16th century Japan, Hawaii artists practice the Western-style raku first developed by Paul Soldner in the 1960’s. The traditional techniques involving rapid heating, removal from the kiln at temperature and rapid cooling remain, however the addition of post-fire reduction creates unpredictable flashing patterns, allowing artists to paint with fire and metallic oxides. The blending of traditional and contemporary techniques reflects Hawaii’s affinity for cultural and artistic fusion and Raku Ho’olaule’a has become a venue for experimentation, creative expression and fine craft executed at the highest level.

 

University of Hawaii BFA graduate Lauren Jo won the Annual Award for Excellence with a series of skeletal structures supported by nichrome armatures; a piece standing in sharp contrast against a field of crisply thrown vessels and figurative, hand-built sculptures. Group’s leader Daven Hee said, “Lauren came from a whole different mindset. We were all thinking about pots. She came in thinking about art. It didn’t make sense to us but she pushed the technical envelope and produced something amazing.”

“It was just a crazy weekend,” said Chad Steve, the student leader from the University of Hawaii. “To see the artists, everyone’s techniques, the jurors, the reaction on people’s faces when they found out they made it into the exhibition. We were all experiencing those things together and we crammed it all into a single weekend. You realize how much the community supports the arts.”

Despite the hardships, Hawaii’s artists prevailed. Once again Raku Ho’olaule’a produced exceptional work, memorable moments and friendships renewed. Organizers have already begun planning the 2012 event, with a focus on cutting cost and changing venues. “It’s part of our survival,” said board member Jackie Mild-Lau. “As artists, we need to grow and change with the times, just like Hawaii Craftsmen. This year’s raku event has become a model for our whole organization.” In the meantime, the artists of Hawaii Craftsmen will continue to build on their current successes and look forward to maintaining an active ceramics community in the islands by preserving local traditions and events as vehicles for future artistic progression.
