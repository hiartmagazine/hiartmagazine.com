THE MERCHANT OF
VENICE

by Bella Freedman  

In April, I was cast in ‘The Merchant of Venice’, this year’s Shakespeare in the Park production in Hilo, Hawaii, directed by Catherine McPherson, which is opening July 7th. I play Launcelot Gobbo, a witty but uneducated servant of the character Shylock. The experience so far has been unique. The actors are all very intelligent and skilled, and as you can imagine, it was a little intimidating at first, being a fourteen year old girl cast as a guy for the first time in a Shakespearean play. However, through the course of the rehearsals, I’ve become very comfortable around understanding the language and working with the amazing cast. Cathy is an excellent director – she’s not afraid to laugh out loud or be honest, and she puts a lot of work into the production. It has been an honor to work with such a talented cast and crew.
If you’re not familiar with the story of ‘The Merchant of Venice’, allow me to fill you in: It takes place in Venice, Italy, and is about Antonio, an unhappy merchant who borrows money from a wealthy but somewhat unpleasant Jewish man, Shylock. Antonio agrees to give Shylock a pound of flesh if he can’t pay Shylock back. The money is actually for his friend Bassanio. Bassanio is trying to impress an intelligent, wealthy woman named Portia, who is struggling to deal with the fact that she cannot select her own husband. Although it may not sound much like a comedy, Cathy tries very hard to emphasize the humor in the story (and certainly succeeds).

Director’s Notes
 
I have always enjoyed the plot of The Merchant of Venice, yet have never seen a production that I felt showed off the humor Shakespeare embedded in the script or the depth of the characters he created. I wanted to create a production that was accessible to a modern audience and emphasized how justice is often blurred by what society expects and tolerates.
 
Balancing the obligations of family and friends, business, and personal dreams and aspirations while living under the constraints of what society expects is one of the themes in this play that should resonate with a modern audience. Mixing in the base human desire of wanting to seek revenge against those who have insulted you because society allows the insults, creates a platform to explore how the boundaries of justice are fickle and ever changing.
 
We have set our production in the very early 1900s, a time when those who did not abide by society’s expectations were ostracized. At this time in history, women were still considered to be the property of their husbands or fathers with few or no rights of their own. There was a general intolerance of members of society who chose not to follow the norms or who were considered to be different or “other.”
 
Shylock’s villainy stems from his choice to allow his desire for revenge to overshadow his abilities as a businessman, and not because of his religious beliefs or ethnic origins. In contrast, the merchant proclaims to be full of Christian benevolence, but only toward those that he considers to be following his beliefs and values. He “goes along with the crowd” and becomes the epitome of a bully who insults, demeans, and humiliates Shylock just because he can get away with it. Shylock’s hate for Antonio stems as much from the public humiliation he has suffered at Antonio’s hands as it does from the apparent hypocrisy he sees in this “Christian” society. 
 
With all this said, one can see why productions of this play can come off a bit heavy. Interspersed amongst all this human drama, however, are some genuinely funny moments. After all, it is a comedy.
 
So sit back, umbrella handy in case of rain, and enjoy!  -- Catherine McPherson

This is Catherine McPherson's 11th Shakespeare in the Park production with the Hilo Community Players. She has been involved with local community theatre since arriving on the Big Island 13 years ago.
 
Catherine holds a BA in Theatre Arts, a Masters in Education, and brings with her over 25 years of experience in community theatre from around the world. She has directed and acted in Australia, teched in Tokyo, stage managed in San Francisco, produced and directed in Hilo, and done nearly everything involved in making theatre possible. A former president of the Hilo Community Players, she now serves as an advisory member of the board of directors.
 
Recently Catherine has been seen onstage in Hilo as Dull, the Constable, in HCP’s Love’s Labours Lost, as a Pick-a-Little Lady in The Music Man at The Palace, and as one of the Wiley Brothers in HCP’s The Taming of the Shrew.
 
Her day job is as a school administrator with the Hawaii Department of Education.
