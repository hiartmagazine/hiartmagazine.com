by Stephen Freedman

 

My romance with dance as a transcendental art form began in late 1973. I had dropped out of college in West Australia a year prior, and on a whim I packed a bag and flew to Indonesia. With only a few hundred dollars to my name I arrived on the island of Bali where I stayed for the better part of a year. Late one night I walked into an open air performance of Kecak (Monkey Dance), a dance performance/chant confabulated from classic Hindu Ramayana myth, in the 1930’s by Balinese choreographers in conjunction with a German composer.


 
Hair stood on the back of my neck as the explosive percussive “Chak, chak, chak…” of the ‘monkey’ chorus rang out, and twenty bare-chested men in black and white checkered sarongs leapt to their feet and shook their hands, in witness to Prince Rama searching for his abducted love, Sita. The guttural vocalizations and stylized angularity of bodily expression transported me from the erudite traditions of European ballet, into the mythic quality of magical reality. The narrative story growled from the throats of luminous dancers as they entered the circle. Abdicating inner restraint, I was drawn into a world of trance, passion and other-worldly possession I’d never imagined possible.

I immersed myself in the culture, learning the language, and taking music and dance lessons from the daughter of one Bali’s most noted choreographers. Following months of instruction, I moved to a tiny village in the unvisited mountains where her father, the great choreographer lived. Nestled in the rainforest, I slept on a straw mat beneath a thatched awning, and continued my dance practice with my teacher’s brothers and ancient father.
One sultry evening I followed as the old man leaned heavily on his cane, descending through terraced rice paddies, to a tiny outdoor temple where practice for an upcoming Legong was taking place. “Legong” is a dance performed exclusively by prepubescent girls, celebrating femininity, elegance and youth. As the ingénue in luminous, flowing sarong, rehearsed her steps in the dappled flame of torchlight, the old man creaked to his feet. He limped toward the young girl, and the gamelan accompanist respectfully silenced his gong. All movement ceased as the girl stepped aside and the hunched ancient, as if by an act of will, raised his chin, straightened his crooked spine, and transmogrified into an embodiment of beauty and innocence, unimagined a moment prior.

Weightless, agile and ageless the old man appeared to float just above the pounded-earth floor, sublime embodiment of the attributes of youth and femininity, gliding through movement, motion and gesture. Then, seconds later, as if it had never happened at all, the music ended, his body hunched forward, and he limped back to his place on the floor, so that the young, hapless student – elevated by his incarnation – might have another opportunity to walk as a spirit, becoming a part of something greater than anything she could possibly have been a moment before.


Soon my stay in Bali came to an end – I was bitten by a sea snake, caught malaria, ran out of money, and simply realized that this wonderland was not my home. Although the sounds of gamelan in the tropical night, and the numinous forms of ritual dance in a mountain village haunted me for years, watching the chimeral attempts of Western students, many far more adept than I was, attempting to mimic Balinese dance persuaded me to end my own personal practice of the form, and slowly the memories faded.

 

 
  
Forty years later, here in the little town of Hilo, Hawaii, dancer/choreographer Shizuno Nasu once again summoned up the ghosts of my ill-spent youth. She organized a collaborative event entitled, Elements: Visual, Sound, Movement at idspace, our tiny venue for alternative art, assembling a team of ten international artists, musicians and performers in a series of eight utterly unique performances, composed, rehearsed and executed in the period of a single month.


The “Visual Element” involved two major canvases by hypnotic colorist, painter Susumu Sakaguchi, scion of Isamu Noguchi, the renowned 20th century sculptor/architect who reshaped America’s relationship with Japanese aesthetics. In the context of performance, Susumu’s canvases alternately became dappled projection screens, fields of visual texture, and clustered galaxies of mind.
 


 

In Japan, the architectural scale monoliths of Tsuneo Taniuchi, punctuate the countryside with acronyms. Arriving in Hilo a day before the first performance, in his bag Tsuneo brought handmade paper shards from his parents’ paper factory, where childhood emersion once birthed an artist’s medium. His iconic installation was at once a maze and a mobile, dancing in response to movement of performers and the trade winds blowing in from Hilo Bay.

 

 
Janne Watson’s images, captured through decades of myopic attention to nature’s mirrors, were projected on walls during performances in staccato rhythms, transforming space and rendering participants as light screens clothed in vestments of shimmering lava, or shipwrecked survivors upon a planet of surreal desolation. Each night the tiny stage expanded through her window into magical surreality.
The “Sound” component began with consummate vocal improvisationalist, Rhiannon – at once performance artist, jazz singer, and high priestess of Babel. Her guttural intonations coaxed and seduced performers and musicians alike into that altered state where individual languages are replaced by the lingua franca of music.
 

 

Evocative, lyrical electric violinist Junji Ishida wove sound sequences, byte upon byte of antique Japanese folklore entwined with rollicking rhythms of Hillbilly. In a call-and-response communion with dancers and vocalist, Junji wove air with color and light, sent shrieking from the wand which was his bow.
 


 

 
Finally, after all other musicians had departed, Chikara Miura squatted before shards of Korean slate, timidly coaxing multiphonics from cracked rock. Animistic gods were drawn from the walls as the rhymes and rhythms of his poetry possessed performers, the gallery dense with the sounds of verdant nature.

 

 
The “Movement Element”, of course was the Shizuno Nasu Dance Institute troupe of dancers, Shizuno, herself, Jenn Eng, Yumiko Inoue and Lucy Braham. Each performance was a unique miniature – an unscripted folk tale inhabited by seers, witches, elves and giants. At times the four dancers were indivisible, each a component of an incarnated myth unfolding before our eyes in a language spoken in movement, gesture, expression and nuance.
   
     


 

But then there were those transcendental moments when Shizuno Nasu alone, occupied the ethers: Malign seductress, Herculean athlete, wounded beast, weaver of Emperor’s clothes, magical woodland creature, arcane witch, or spirit of life and death.
 
   
 
As the series of eight performances culminated, and the cheering crowd stood in ovation, witness to a transformative creative experience, this five foot tall woman bowed and presented her ensemble of collaborators; and in her shadow, I saw that ancient choreographer of Bali limp back to his seat in the flashing torch lit tropic night, offering us opportunity to become a part of something greater than we could possibly have been just a moment before.
