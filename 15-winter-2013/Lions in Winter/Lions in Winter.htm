“Lee Chesney/Oliver Jackson/Albert Paley: mono prints and other works”, October - November 2012 at idspace, celebrated the Visiting Artist program and the international focus of a relatively obscure department printmaking studio that was established by the late Wayne A. Miyamoto (2010), Professor and Chair of the Art Department program at the University of Hawaii at Hilo.

As a Visiting Assistant Professor, please note: visiting assistant … in 1982 Miyamoto established a foundation account Friends of UHHART:

“The purpose of UHHART shall be to enhance the educational mission of the Art Discipline of the University of Hawaii at Hilo as well as the cultural environment of the Islands of Hawaii; encourage interaction between community members, faculty, and students in the interest of artistic and cultural development; and to solicit the active support of the community in the sustenance of a vigorous Art Discipline at the University of Hawaii at Hilo so that the program will better serve students and residents of the Island.”

As a devotee of the medium, the Printmaking Studio at UHH became the piko of the department program in art. Miyamoto was also a very fine painter and an exceptional draftsman. Why printmaking became the central thrust for the department had much to do with managing limited resources in a way that would generate the most return in support of professional training for UHH students, and to make this available to both art and non-art majors.

The powerful isolation of the Hawaiian archipelago limits real-time exposure to contemporary international developments in the visual arts. Students attending university to study art in the fledgling program were often richly immersed in the local cultural experience but impoverished when it came to exposure to the plastic arts: drawing, painting, and printmaking. The nearest art museum, 300 miles away on the island of Oahu, the Honolulu Academy of Art, was too distant to fill the void. How to bridge this gap in art training; how to bring students face-to-face with original works of art created by more experienced masters in the craft was a real dilemma.

 
Miyamoto’s brilliant resolution was to develop a Campus Center Gallery exhibition program. In December 2012, the department hosted the 30th year of Pacific States National Biennial (PSBN) print exhibition. The show juried by Karen Kunc, an internationally recognized and highly acclaimed printmaker was Artist-In-Residence in the department print studio November 4- 11, 2012. During the week Kunc created a new wood block multi color print utilizing a reduction process that she has mastered; interacted with the students and provided a free public lecture.

By the time the PSBN closes in April 2013, engaged students will have had the opportunity to literally handle and view up close and personal, the strongest contemporary original prints selected by our juror for a national exhibition that attracts up to 300 entries per show.

The value of this informal practicum, learning how to receive (unpacking) work, matting and framing; installation of the exhibition, developing a catalog, etc…is immeasurable. The associated visiting artist program provides additional opportunity to engage; talk shop about art making in general. The floodgates to critical, relevant, and current visual thinking was opened through the avenue of printmaking and art department sponsored Campus Center Gallery exhibitions and residencies.


 Lee Chesney
The idspace exhibition was especially poignant because Lee Chesney(1920), Professor Emeritus University of Hawai’i at Manoa was Wayne Miyamoto’s graduate art school mentor. Oliver Jackson(1935), Professor Emeritus, California State University, Sacramento, CA sometime in the 70’s received an invitation from Chesney to come to UH Manoa as a visiting artist-in-residence scholar. The graduate students had nominated Jackson on the basis of notices that he was receiving at the time in the established art press (Art in America, Art News, etc…). Miyamoto drew the short straw to pick Jackson up from the airport and this was the start of a lifelong friendship between two equally strong personalities brilliantly devoted to scholarship.

In the fall of 2009 Miyamoto submitted his last grant proposal to the Hawaii Community Foundation Laila Twigg-Smith Art Fund seeking support to bring Albert Paley (1944) to the UHH, print studio as an Artist-in-Residence. Paley, who holds an Endowed Chair, College of Imaging Arts and Sciences, Rochester Institute of Technology, had already by this time been selected by the Hawaii State Foundation on Culture and the Arts (SFCA) to execute a major sculpture for the university campus. Miyamoto was part of the UHH Art Advisory Committee that worked hand-in-hand with the SFCA to review the sculpture commission project that started as an Open Call for Artists March 24, 2009 through April 23, 2009 and attracted 330 Hawaii, Continental US, and International entries. The committee which had a mandate to provide priority consideration for Hawaii based artists ultimately chose Albert Paley as the most qualified for that particular project.

The Hawaii Community Foundation grant supporting the 2010 Paley residency was helped tremendously along by attention given by Miyamoto to details surrounding a forthcoming Howard and Yoneko Droste Bequest to the Art Department. The bequest was publically announced in August 2010, six months after his passing.

When I first met Wayne at the art department College Hall studio on the old upper campus in the fall of 1984 he was on a ladder with hammer in hand personally attending to a print studio repair. The attention to detail and effort to set things in place for the future was continuous and remains today a powerful legacy supporting the program and the Chesney/Jackson/Paley idspace exhibition.

The collective biographies and international record of exhibitions, awards; collections holding works by Chesney, Jackson, and Paley include the Bibliothéque Nationale, Paris France; the Victoria and Albert Museum, London, England; Tokoyo University of the Arts and the Tokoyo Museum of Modern Art, Tokoyo, Japan; the National Gallery of Art, Stockholm, Sweden; the Tate Gallery, London, England; and the United States Embassy, Bonn, Germany; The High Museum of Art, Atlanta, GA; Metropolitan Museum of Art, New York; Museum of Contemporary Art, Chicago, IL; Museum of Fine Arts, Boston; Museum of Modern Art, NY; San Francisco Museum of Modern Art, San Francisco, CA; Seattle Art Museum, Seattle, WA; American Craft Museum, New York, NY; Art Gallery of Western Australia, Perth, Australia; The British Museum London, England; The Columbus Museum of Art, Columbus, Ohio; Cooper-Hewitt, Smithsonian Institution, Washington, DC; The Corcoran Gallery, Washington, DC; Detroit Institute of Arts, Detroit, Michigan; The Fitzwilliam Museum, Cambridge University, England; International Sculpture Center’s Collection II; Minneapolis Institute of Arts, Minneapolis, Minnesota; Philadelphia Museum of Art, Philadelphia, Pennsylvania; Yale University Art Gallery, New Haven, Connecticut – provides a sampling.

This extraordinary collection of recent works (some on loan from the University of Hawaii at Hilo Permanent Collection of Prints and Drawings) by a constellation of contemporary master artists who have spent decades honing their craft was a manifestation of the vision and unrelenting lifetime of service to the muse to that was set in motion by Professor Wayne A. Miyamoto.
