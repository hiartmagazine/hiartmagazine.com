Helena Kucharz – Myczkowce
(translated from audio recording)

In ’45 on the 2nd of October the army came here and was stationed in Lesko with all their transport; they had horses there. They came to get some fodder for them: hay, grain and oats. And the farmers say: "Our village is ruined, the fields have gone wild from not being ploughed, go to the next door village where there are locals, they weren’t displaced and there you’ll get something quickly." So they went to Bereźnica. When they got there, the villagers were killing a calf belonging to one of Bandera’s men in the middle of the village and roasting the meat for future supplies in the forest. When they saw that the Polish army was coming they opened fire on them, and injured a soldier. It ended with that, the army retreated and returned to Lesko.

In Lesko – I was small, I can’t remember, but dad used to say that the commanding officer gave an order and in an instant two tanks came here, stood by the San and gave two shots there and there. Two army cars went to those places and we could already see flames. A few people died there; they burned down houses. That was on Tuesday, because they were going from Lesko by carts and they didn’t go there because they were scared; they stayed the night at my neighbour’s house, at our friends. And on Wednesday they came to Myczkowce to get their revenge. Our house was the first one on entering the village; it wasn’t our own because our previous one was taken apart. We lived in a farm building where the paint shop was. It was a wooden house.

On the other side of the San river Nowak had a farm, on this side were the manor buildings. There were also buildings there; lots of people lived there, they would come and have a place to stay. Bandera’s men went there and didn’t spare anyone, Cycek Antoni and Stefan Dydacki, who had only just come back from Germany and wasn’t aware of the danger; he didn’t know what Bandera’s men were or what they were capable of. They came to this older man and wanted to drag him outside. He was a lively man though and struggled his way out and was left alone but they took the other two and in a moment they heard shots. They shot him practically on his doorstep. They crossed the ford through the San.

Józef Płaksa lived on the other side. He takes a look around and his wife comes up to him and says someone’s going across the river. He replies that maybe the Polish army is coming back from action. He looks around and then they come to him; Bandera’s men. They shot him straight away on the meadow. Then they came to us, surrounding the first house. I was 12. At that moment people get an amazing sense of power; I don’t know if any of my peers would have done the same thing in my situation. My father was lying asleep on his bed, dinner was being cooked, there were eight of us in the family. Gran was sitting on her bed; my sister was one-and-a-half years old. Mum told me to change her skirt which was hanging on the stove. Suddenly we hear from behind the wall, shots. Mum immediately went to wake Dad up: Franek, get up, it’s Bandera’s lot! Dad jumped out of bed and escaped outside.

This was all happening around one building with no stables but a kind of shed which he had prepared for the cows for the winter months. As soon as he ran out and saw that there are so many of Bandera’s men he turned around but it was very dark and it was raining and kind of hanging drizzle. Bandera’s mob didn’t know the area, and my father knew where to go and fled for the stables. He got there and one of Bandera’s men was right behind him and started to fire. When he started firing a horse which was tied by the doors started jumping, and he was too scared to go on because he didn’t want to be trampled. Dad ran to the window, broke it open while getting injured at the same time. The bullet entered him here; it damaged a tendon and later he had contracture of his arm. He climbed out of the window; the bandit had gone around, there were some barbed-wire entanglements because the police had been there. Later, when Bandera’s men started raiding the police station they took them from here for reinforcement to Olszanica, because it’s a different story if you have bigger groups rather than police stations with just a few people in them.

I jumped up onto the table, and onto the stove because I wanted to take the baby’s vest and at once I heard a shot at the window. Had there been electric light he wouldn’t have missed but we had a paraffin lamp; it was dark inside. He injured Gran badly; she fell onto the floor and started to cry in agony and then he fired again into the other window at me. I swear I heard the bullet whistle passed me around a foot in front of me. I only saw shards fly off from the stove. I knew that when they came they are going to set the place on fire because every night you could see the flames from other fires in the distance; they would set houses on fire and murder people and I realized that the same fate had come to us; what we had feared most.

I dashed to my bed where there were two pillows and a quilt; I bent over and took the baby and everything else outside. Today I wouldn’t have done that. I hauled everything out and I don’t know; whether it was God deciding that my time had not yet come, because I can’t explain it any other way, because I am sure that Bandera’s lot saw me. They were very aggravated by the whole situation; you could see it. They had nervous voices when giving orders when they would talk to each other. There was a very large ash tree whose branches would spread out and I went there. I threw aside the two pillows because I thought out here they won’t burn when they set the house on fire but I took the quilt, knowing that when I went to sleep the baby had to be wrapped up. I could die but at least the baby would survive. We crawled under the barbed wire; we knew the way because we used to go that way when we were kids. The wire caught the baby and I had to calm her down: "Quiet, Józia, quiet now, don’t cry…"

And so I went into the garden and sat under the nut tree. It was the 3rd October; it was wet and wet leaves were falling from the tree. And I’m sitting there. The quilt was bright pink on one side; on the other a dark crimson. I thought to myself that the darker colour will be harder to spot in the dark so I took the quilt and started to turn it around and wrap the baby in it. As I started a whole bunch of leaves fell; I had moved a nearby hedge. At that moment – something which I didn’t know about then – one of Bandera’s men was standing right behind me, he came closer and I heard the click of his gun being taken off the safety catch. He cried out nervously: "Who’s there?" So I think to myself: "I escaped here but where to now? If I answer him, he’ll know where I am, fire off a round and kill me and if I don’t say anything he’ll fire one off just to make sure and seriously injure me and I’ll be in agony just like my Gran a moment ago." But I couldn’t bring myself to say anything; my throat was so tense. I still get anxious even now just thinking about it, it all comes flooding back as if I find myself in the same situation again. I clenched my fists, maybe it’s a slightly childish thing to do, just like going to the dentist’s and then clenching your fists so you think it won’t hurt as much.

I took the baby close to me and I sat deadly still, and waited for what seemed to be an eternity. And this man from Bandera’s mob shouts out again: "Who’s there?!" At the same time just try and imagine if it was not my destiny determined by God, a miracle, that God had decided that my time hadn’t come yet; at the same time when the man asked who was there was another one of Bandera’s men just behind him who was sure that the question was directed at him so he replies: "’m going into the house." So one of them went one way, the second the other. I pinched myself just to make sure I was still alive. Anyone who hasn’t gone through something like that cannot begin to explain what it’s like.

He went to our quarters and took the paraffin lamp. When I left the house with the baby I’m sure there was a sack of potatoes, earlier there were sacks which Gran would stitch together with white cloth. My grandfather had white cloth underwear; he got out of bed, he was lying on the floor. I went over him and left into the garden. I didn’t see my mother. Dad got back to the house after everything was over. They burnt down the house and went further into the village, where they shot Władek Tubulewski. Past the wall where we were living shots were fired and the first to go was Stanisław Tubulewski, 42 years old, with four children.

Later I thought to myself: "Maybe there’s someone injured in the house and I won’t be able to save them because the house is on fire." My father sat in the garden injured; he went slowly into our quarters. He stumbled upon my mother and shook her but she was already dead. He went along to Gran’s bed but she too was already lying dead on the ground. There was smoke everywhere, blood pouring out, the whole incident, stress. And dad says that he’s scared that he’s about to lose consciousness so he’ll fall over and burn along with the house. Flames were coming up to the door; there was no other way out. He managed to break open a window and went into the garden again. Dad saved us four children. My sister was one and a half. She’s now a retired school teacher; she lives in Sanok. I said to her, "Józia, come with us," because we were going to the Ukraine to where we used to live, because that’s where she was born. But she said, "I can’t." I didn’t have a childhood thanks to them. As they say: "You can forgive but you can’t forget; you have to remember." Twelve people died then.
